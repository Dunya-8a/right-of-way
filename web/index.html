RIGHT OF WAY

An agent-to-agent right-of-way protocol for physical swarms — demoed in orbit

WHAT YOU'RE WATCHING

Two satellites are predicted to pass dangerously close. There is no air-traffic control in space — in real life, operators coordinate close approaches by email (in 2019, SpaceX missed ESA's messages because of a paging bug). Here, each satellite has an AI agent. They negotiate who moves, in plain English, over the channel on the right — and a deterministic physics engine referees every deal.

WAIT — SATELLITES HAVE FUEL?

Yes. Most satellites carry a small tank of propellant for tiny thrusters. Firing them is called a burn. The lifetime supply is measured as Δv (delta-v) — the total change in velocity, in meters per second, the tank can still produce. A typical dodge costs 0.01–0.05% of orbital speed; this demo's burns are 10–45 m/s.

Why it matters: fuel is mission lifetime. Every dodge you make is months of operational life you don't get back — which is exactly why nobody wants to be the one who moves, and why this is a negotiation, not a calculation. When the tank hits zero (or the satellite is dead, like a defunct nanosat), it physically cannot move — someone else must.

WHO HAS RIGHT OF WAY?

Honest answer: there is no agreed standard — that's the gap this project pokes at. The demo models a right-of-way policy as a priority number (higher outranks lower — think crewed spacecraft > science missions > mega-constellation sat #4,412 > debris). The norm says the lower-priority party dodges. The interesting moments are when the norm can't hold: the lower-priority satellite is out of fuel, or its operator won't answer — and the agents have to talk their way to who burns instead.

THE PHYSICS REFEREE

The agents can argue, bargain — even lie. It doesn't matter: no agreement counts until a deterministic orbital-mechanics engine confirms the proposed burn actually clears the encounter and fits the mover's fuel budget. After every burn it re-scans the sky — if the dodge created a new close approach, the agents are thrown back to the table. And claims get audited: say "I can't move" while the referee's ground truth shows a full tank, and the deal is voided and the burn lands on you. The judge is physics, not vibes.
